## Section-by-section extracted content

Paraphrased for accuracy and brevity; short phrases are quoted only
where the exact wording matters (a defined term, a specific figure, an
acronym). Full manual prose is not reproduced here.

**Understanding Coast Guard Searches (p.89–~90).** In a normal
distress, drift with the current using a sea anchor rather than trying
to move away from it — a motorized lifeboat only carries ~24 hours of
fuel at 6 knots, rowing is slow and energy-costly, and the standing lug
sail rig is a poor sailing rig. Staying near the last known position
maximizes rescue chances. The USCG's **AMVER** (Automated Merchant
Vessel Report) system tracks nearby merchant vessels' positions so the
Coast Guard can redirect ships to a disaster scene; mariners are legally
and customarily required to assist vessels in distress.

**Abandoning Ship / PFDs (p.~90–91).** Introduces PFD = Personal
Flotation Device, informally "life preserver." The most common
USCG/SOLAS Type I is a vest-style jacket with a large strap around the
back, hooking at the front, sometimes with an upper strap too; an adult
PFD can be adjusted for a child in an emergency. Care requirements:
don't store near open flame/smoking areas, don't use as a seat/pillow/
footrest, must self-right an unconscious wearer face-up, must be
maintained and readily accessible (commonly stowed in cabins/on deck,
spares on the bridge/control rooms). Required features: a signaling
whistle on a cord, a small water-activated light, reflective tape, and
each unit stenciled with the vessel's name. Required color: **"Indian
orange, International orange, or Munsell"** (a specific, named color
requirement, not just "any bright orange").

**Immersion Suits (p.92).** Required by SOLAS on ships that carry them;
described as the best defense against hypothermia and increases cold-
water survival time. Key claim: *"will not allow your body temperature
to fall more than 2°-5° after 6 hours in water between 0°C-2°C in calm
water."* **This is a discrepancy worth flagging, not resolving**: the
already-live CFR-sourced question (`q-cfr-suit-jumpheight`'s sibling
facts, ultimately from 46 CFR 160.171-9(c)(2)) states a hard cap of "no
more than 2°C" drop, while this manual passage reads as a range ("2°-5°")
— a looser claim. Also covers: most suits have an integrated buoyancy
pillow (inflated via oral tube) that makes wearing a PFD with the suit
inappropriate (may obstruct the pillow) — unless the suit lacks that
pillow, in which case a PFD is fine; an immersion suit may not
self-right an unconscious wearer face-up (unlike some PFDs); zipper
should be lubricated with bee's wax or paraffin; repairs only at an
approved shore facility, otherwise replace; donning time: *"should be
able to get into their immersion suit in less than 2 minutes"* (matches
the already-live CFR-sourced 2-minute figure); suit should be carried,
not worn, until reaching the deck edge; other names include "Survival
Suit" and "Gumby Suit"; explicitly distinct from a wet suit, dry suit, or
Anti-Exposure suit.

**Anti-Exposure Suit (p.93).** Trades some of the immersion suit's
protection for much better mobility/dexterity; meant to be worn *with* a
lifejacket or buoyant work vest, unlike most immersion suits. Has
pockets for gear (portable VHF, flashlight, etc.). Commonly used by
rescue boat crews (the manual specifies a typical crew size of 3).
Alternate names: "Mustang suit" (after an original manufacturer) and,
for a coat-only version, "float coat."

**Entering the Water from a Height (p.93).** Put on extra wind/
waterproof clothing with a hood and gloves if time allows. Secure all
suit/jacket ties before jumping — an unsecured suit risks head injury on
impact. Key figures: *"life jackets, immersion suits, and anti exposure
suits will all allow the user to jump from a height of at least 4.5
meters without any damage to the suit/jacket"* (matches the already-live
CFR figure exactly). Separately, general jump-height guidance: stay
below 30 feet if possible, with *"below fifteen feet is ideal"* (matches
the already-live `q-quiz1a-preferred-jump-height` answer of "15 feet or
less" exactly) — and a new figure not currently live anywhere: jumping
from **higher than 30 feet will probably cause injury**. Secure loose
items and empty pockets of sharp objects before jumping.

**Assume the Stepping Off Position (p.94).** A 5-step sequence: (1)
block nose and mouth with one hand; (2) cross the other hand over the
chest, gripping the lifejacket firmly; (3) look down to check what's
below; (4) look straight ahead, cross ankles, keep elbows at your sides,
step off, and hold the position until you resurface; (5) board the
survival craft as soon as possible. This is more detailed than, and
phrased differently from, the currently-live
`q-quiz6-abandonship-jump-procedure` ("cross your arms and legs, and
make sure there's no debris below") — compatible in spirit, but not a
verbatim match; a good candidate for a source-verified refinement rather
than a flat confirmation.

**Saving Your Strength (p.94).** After entering the water: swim clear
of the ship but stay in the immediate area, stay calm, look for other
survivors and craft, huddle for warmth/morale, signal without wasting
energy, and swim slowly rather than thrashing — thrashing both wastes
heat and strength. References the H.E.L.P. and HUDDLE positions (detailed
later in the batch) and notes the life jacket should support a face-up
float.

**Swimming for Survival (p.95).** Wave-slap protection: cup hands over
nose/mouth. Surf and currents: small waves close to shore can be
body-surfed; for large waves, swim under them between crests, covering
nose/mouth and holding your breath, or dive under if you have no PFD and
are a strong swimmer. For rip/dangerous currents: *"don't [fight it].
Swim parallel to the shore or diagonally [across] the current"* — head
toward shore only once clear of the current. This is compatible with,
but does not itself state, the already-live `q-quiz6-swim-strongcurrent-
45deg` question's specific "45 degrees" figure — the manual excerpt here
confirms the *direction* strategy (diagonal, not head-on) without
independently confirming the exact angle.

**Swimming Through Burning Oil (p.96).** Jump at the bow or stern, on
the windward side, if the water is covered in burning oil — matches the
already-live `q-quiz6-oilfire-waterentry` answer exactly. If you must
swim through oil/debris: remove your life jacket and shoes (keep other
clothing on for protection), swim underwater as much as possible, and
when surfacing, beat the oil away from your face with circular arm
motions while turning your back to the flame before diving again, then
swim to windward, underwater.

**Floating With No Life Jacket (p.96–97).** If no PFD and no debris to
hold: (1) trapped-air-in-clothing method — lie back, keep chin above
water, minimize thrashing to avoid losing trapped air; (2) back-float
method — relax limbs, keep lungs full, expect a gulp-breathe-sink-rise
rhythm rather than staying fully surfaced continuously.

**Tread and Scull (p.97).** Single scissors kick technique for treading
(legs together, knees bent, scissor motion) plus a hand-sculling
technique (small downward circular hand motions, palms down) — the
manual gives a labeled 6-step "how to tread water efficiently" diagram
sequence combining both.

**Clothing for flotation (p.98).** Only remove clothing that weighs you
down or that can be repurposed for flotation. Shirt method: blow air
between the second and third button to trap air in the back, button the
collar tight, take a breath, bend head forward. Pants method: remove
while jellyfish-floating, tie both legs near the cuffs, zip up, splash
air into the open waistband while holding it closed with one hand, then
slip your head between the tied pant legs.

**Man Overboard (p.99–100).** Reasons crew go overboard: accidents
(losing footing, being swept by waves, pulled by mooring lines, falling
from an accommodation ladder), climbing/sitting on railings, alcohol/
drugs, low visibility or rough seas. A person overboard should *not* try
to swim after the ship. Immediate crew response: throw ring buoys
(with lifelines; lighted at night; smoke signal by day is also helpful),
hail "Man Overboard, Port (or Starboard) Side" to the wheelhouse,
lookouts go high and keep continuous eyes/pointing on the person's
position. The mate on watch turns the stern *away* from the person to
reduce propeller-strike risk. **The Williamson Turn** (described as the
most commonly used shipboard MOB maneuver): (1) put the rudder hard over
toward the side the person fell; (2) after the heading has changed 60°
from the original course, put the rudder hard over to the opposite side;
(3) when the heading is 20° short of the reciprocal course, bring the
rudder amidships. The manual's own worked example (original course 110°,
reciprocal 290°) states the rudder goes amidships at *"310 degrees (i.e.
20 degrees short of 290 degrees)"* — **this arithmetic doesn't check
out** (290° − 20° = 270°, not 310°); flagged as a likely OCR/transcription
error in the sampled figure, not confirmed, and should not be treated as
a verified number without checking the original page directly. The
60°-first-phase figure, however, matches the already-live
`q-quiz6-williamsonturn-manoverboard` question exactly (that question is
tagged `chapter-6-being-rescued`, not Chapter 4 — a cross-chapter overlap
worth knowing, same pattern as the immersion-suit CFR questions tagged to
Chapter 1). Recovery guidance: send a swimmer with a lifeline/PFD/suit if
the victim can't help themselves; if using a lifeboat, approach nearly
into the wind with the wind off the bow, placing the victim to leeward
for a calmer pickup; approach a drowning victim by the most direct route.

**Cold Water Survival / Hypothermia (p.100–107).** This is by far the
largest and most detailed section in the batch. Key content, organized
loosely:

- *Definitions and thresholds*: normal body temperature 98.6°F;
  hypothermia = loss of body heat. Air-temperature hypothermia risk
  range given as *"between 30° and 50°F"* (an air-temperature figure,
  distinct from water-immersion figures below). Wind chill significantly
  worsens the effect (full wind-chill chart included in the source).
- *Water immersion onset*: hypothermia "can begin to develop in as little
  as ten to fifteen minutes" in cold water, but "depending on temperature
  and wind chill, it can take several hours." States a person in water
  **"70°F and lower"** generally cannot generate enough heat to keep up
  with losses — this matches the already-live
  `q-quiz1a-hypothermia-water-temp` answer ("70°") exactly.
- *Cold-water swimming exhaustion*: a good swimmer in 45°F water becomes
  completely exhausted after only about 10 minutes of swimming, due to
  loss of muscle/sensory control (distinct from core-temperature
  hypothermia — described as a separate danger).
- *Alcohol*: explicitly does not prevent hypothermia and increases risk.
- *Cold Shock Response — a 4-stage progression* (this is a clean,
  well-structured chart, worth close attention): **(1) Cold Shock
  Response**, lasts ~1 minute, automatic gasp reflex risking water
  inhalation, remaining calm is described as key; **(2) Cold
  Incapacitation**, 5–15 minutes, decreasing blood flow, loss of ability
  to move extremities or stay afloat; **(3) Circum-Rescue Collapse**, can
  occur before, during, or after rescue, symptoms range from fainting to
  death; **(4) Hypothermia**, described here as taking "up to 30 minutes"
  to occur. **Note**: this "up to 30 minutes" onset figure sits somewhat
  differently from the earlier "10–15 minutes... up to several hours"
  framing a few pages prior — both appear in the source, and this intake
  is not reconciling them into one number; they may describe different
  severities/conditions, but that should be confirmed rather than
  assumed. This chart directly supports the already-live `q-surv-5`,
  `q-surv-6`, and `q-surv-7` questions (cold shock response definition,
  distinguishing it from hypothermia, and the first-minute danger).
- *Symptoms*: violent shivering escalating to shivering stopping,
  blue-grey/bluish skin, slow/difficult breathing, uncoordinated
  movement, loss of hand/foot function, muscle spasms, "drunk or
  drugged" appearance, mental confusion/forgetfulness, personality
  change, poor decision-making, loss of consciousness, appearing dead.
- *Clinical classification by core temperature*: Not hypothermic (>35°C/
  95°F); Mild (35–32°C/95–90°F, shivering, good rewarming prognosis);
  Moderate (32–28°C/90–82°F, shivering stops, limited rewarming
  prognosis); Severe (<28°C/82°F, unable to rewarm without external
  help, risk of cardiac arrest with rough handling). Rectal temperature
  is called out as the only reliable measurement (oral is unreliable
  since the mouth is a cold-water access point). Recovery is described
  as possible down to 18°C (64.4°F) core temperature.
- *Three practical types (by symptom, when no thermometer is available)*:
  **Mild** (~just under 95°F — slurred speech, conscious but sluggish,
  strong shivering); **Moderate** (82–89°F — shivering stops,
  consciousness clouds, slow/hard-to-detect breathing); **Severe** (≤82°F
  — very confused, fixed/dilated pupils, "drunk or drugged" behavior,
  approaching or at unconsciousness). Explicit guidance: when in doubt,
  treat for severe.
- *First aid, mild*: move to warmth, let the body rewarm naturally,
  monitor for worsening, treat as severe if in doubt.
- *First aid, moderate/severe*: minimal, gentle handling (the heart is
  unstable — don't let the victim exert themselves, you do the moving);
  remove wet clothing gently (cutting it off is acceptable to avoid
  movement); rewarm gently, chest/sides/groin first (these are named as
  the greatest heat-loss areas); wrap in a warm dry blanket; a rescuer
  may share body heat in a sleeping bag/blanket; warm rescuer breath into
  the victim's mouth timed with their own inhalation (usable even if the
  victim is breathing on their own; start immediately if they are not
  breathing); warm liquids only if the victim is conscious; **never**
  give alcohol (increases heat loss); transport to medical care as
  gently and quickly as possible. A notable persistence point: victims
  have fully recovered after up to 44 minutes with no apparent life
  signs and a core temperature as low as 64°F — don't give up early.
- *H.E.L.P. position* (Heat Escape Lessening Posture): cross arms close
  to the chest, cross and extend legs downward to help float face-up
  (since some PFDs may otherwise turn a person in this position face
  down); protects the head, neck, chest sides, and groin (named as the
  greatest heat-loss areas); claimed to increase survival time by up to
  50%.
- *HUDDLE*: group survivors with sides touching for the same heat-loss
  protection, plus a larger visual target for rescuers and morale
  benefit.

**Organizing for Survival (p.108).** Leadership and morale framed as
directly linked ("good leadership creates high morale"). Command order:
senior ship's officer present, else senior lifeboat-endorsed crew
member, else an elected leader. Leader's responsibilities: explain
actions to reduce worry/doubt, project calm and firm leadership, point to
available resources (shelter, flotation, food, water, signaling,
companionship) to build confidence. Establishing a routine: collect
sharp objects/potential weapons, take a roll call, assign duties —
**ration keeper, lookouts, repair party, bailers, first-aid persons,
signaling**. Keep minds occupied but avoid unnecessary/overexerting work.

**Survival on the Sea (p.109).** After organizing: confirm the sea
anchor is set on every craft with an anchor watch; watch for breaking
seas in heavy weather to warn of incoming water and prevent the drogue
line from fouling (which risks broaching — turning sideways to wind/
seas — and capsizing). Capsize recovery: for a lifeboat, everyone moves
to one side and uses grab rails to right it; for a raft, righting straps
are used. In heavy seas, if movement is necessary, keep the wind/seas
broad on the bow (about 30–40°) for steering control while minimizing
broaching risk; prefer streaming the drogue and drifting bow-into-the-
weather over trying to make headway, unless drifting onto a hazard.

**Preserve Body Fluids / Avoid Seasickness (p.110).** Seasickness is
described as nearly universal in a liferaft and worth actively avoiding
since vomiting wastes body fluids that are hard to replace. Take
anti-seasickness medication before abandoning ship if possible, or as
soon as everyone is aboard the craft otherwise.

**Urinate Soon After Boarding (p.110).** Urinate within 2 hours of
boarding if not done shortly before. Trauma can make urination
difficult; two suggested techniques (pouring seawater cup-to-cup nearby,
or hanging over the side with the lower body in water) may help. Dark,
thick urine after several days without water is described as a normal
dehydration response, not alarming on its own.

**Dealing with Cold / Hot Environment (p.111).** Cold: keep warm/dry,
close canopy curtains or lifeboat doors, huddle, do small isometric
exercises (clenching fists, stretching, wiggling extremities) to
circulate blood without wasting energy, and rotate the lookout position
to limit any one person's cold exposure. Hot: maintain airflow (may
require repositioning the sea anchor), avoid sunburn, minimize exertion
to reduce sweat/fluid loss, keep the raft's exterior wet, and wet
clothing with seawater during the day.

**Drinking Water (p.112–113).** Survival-without-water figures: the
body can survive 40+ days on as little as 11 oz of water/day (no food);
2–3 oz/day can maintain health for up to 10 days; without any water,
delirium sets in around 4 days, death in 8–12 days. **No water in the
first 24 hours** (the body is already saturated; extra water is mostly
wasted as urine) — except for a survivor who has lost blood or been
burned, and only if conscious. After 24 hours: a full ration of **1 pint
per person per day**, split into three equal doses at sunrise, midday,
and sunset. Rain collection: boat cover/buckets in an open lifeboat;
built-in canopy catchment (enclosed lifeboats/liferafts) feeding storage
bags, with a caution that early rainwater may carry dried salt spray
from the canopy. Condensation: collected with a dedicated clean
(salt-free) cellulose sponge, squeezed out to drink or store — broadly
consistent with, though more specific than, the already-live
`q-quiz6-condensation-collect-drink` question (which allows "a sponge or
a t-shirt").

**Snow / Ice, and the seawater/urine warning (p.113).** Old, weathered
sea ice (bluish, smooth rounded corners) is generally safe; freshly
formed "salt ice" (gray, milky) is not and should not be eaten. Don't eat
snow/ice if already near hypothermia — let it melt and warm first.
**Never** mix or substitute salt water, urine, or animal fluids for
fresh water — drinking seawater worsens thirst, accelerates fluid loss
via the kidneys/intestines, and can cause delirium, convulsions, and
death.

**Emergency Provisions / Food (p.113–115).** Survival-craft rations are
carbohydrate/vitamin-heavy, salt-free, and low-protein (protein and salt
both cost extra body fluid to process). No food in the first 24 hours.
Craft rations provide roughly **10,000 kJ (2,400 cal)** per occupant, per
the craft's rated capacity. Never eat without available water — the body
needs water to digest food, and eating without it can be fatal. Seafood
specifically needs 2–3× the normal water ration to digest safely; skip
it if that margin isn't available. Falling back on stored body fat is
described as viable (roughly 1 lb of fat ≈ 2 meals' worth of energy),
more so if the survivor stays calm and avoids overexertion or extreme
temperatures. Fish: open-ocean fish are generally safe; near-shore fish
can be poisonous (named examples: puffer, porcupine, parrotfish — look
for self-inflating or spined/bristled fish as warning signs). Organs,
blood, eyes are edible/useful; stomach contents of large fish may
contain edible smaller fish. Catching methods: the provided fishing kit,
improvised spearing with a knife lashed to an oar/boat hook, or
attracting flying fish with a light at night. Juice can be extracted from
excess catch by wringing wrapped flesh; fish can be sun-dried in strips
for preservation — but uncleaned fish spoils in about half a day, so
clean and eat/dry immediately. Turtles: meat/blood/juice edible, caution
after decapitation (head can still bite, claws can scratch), fat can be
rendered into oil. Seaweed: tough, salty, hard to digest — only worth
eating with ample water; often hosts edible crabs/shrimp/small fish
(shake off jellyfish first). Birds: blood and liver edible; can be
grabbed if they land on the craft, or caught with a baited hook dragged
behind the craft; feathers useful as fishing lures; birds diving on a
school indicate nearby fish. Two survival anecdotes are cited: a
133-day Atlantic drift survival on fish and rainwater, and an 83-day raft
survival supported by 25+ caught birds.

**Looking for Land (p.115–117).** Dense, white, flat-based, fluffy
cumulus clouds forming over an area (rather than covering it broadly)
can indicate nearby land, since land air rising creates them.

**Beaching Survival Craft (p.116–117).** Avoid a surf landing unless
necessary — professional rescue crews train and equip for this
specifically, conditions merchant-vessel survival craft and crews
usually lack (especially with children/elderly aboard). Put a life
jacket back on before entering surf. Look for a break in a reef (near a
stream mouth, a visible depression, or where waves visibly continue
toward shore without breaking — indicating a clearer channel). Study the
wave pattern before committing; surf always looks calmer from the craft
than it is. Pulling boat (rowed): approach stern-first, bow to sea,
rowing to meet each breaking wave; rig the sweep and stow the rudder for
control while backing in; use the drogue between breakers, slacking the
trip line to steady the boat through each wave crest. Power boat: engine
off, use oars and the sweep (not the rudder), same stern-first approach,
drogue tripped between breakers. Rafts: avoid at night unless necessary
(sea anchor + wait for daylight is preferred); rocks/coral can puncture
a raft; open both canopy curtains to reduce wind resistance if working
against the wind.

**Breezes Near Land (p.117, continuing past the missing page).** Daytime
land heating creates a rising-air/onshore "sea breeze" (cooler air moving
from water to replace it), which can exceed 15 knots and combine with
the prevailing wind; this pattern is tied to cumulus cloud formation
described earlier. A survivor timing a raft landing should take in the
sea anchor as this onshore breeze develops (typically after sunrise) and
stream it again at night to avoid drifting away from shore.

**Finding Food and Water ashore (p.119, likely continuing from the
missing page).** Fresh water may be found by digging roughly 100 yards
above the high-tide line — dig only about a foot past where water first
seeps in (fresh water floats above salt water in the sand), scoop from
the top, and try another spot if it's still too salty. Also mentions
streams, rivers, ponds, melting snow/ice, and boiling water of uncertain
purity before drinking. Food sources ashore are referenced back to the
same wildlife categories described earlier, plus edible wild plants "if
you know what to look for" (no specific plant guidance given in the
sampled text).
