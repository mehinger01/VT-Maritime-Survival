## Section-by-section extracted content

### Rescued at Last! (p.134, top portion only)

Once spotted, the ordeal is not necessarily over — there are still
problems to overcome. An aircraft may spot the survival craft and
report position to the Coast Guard, but it may be some time before a
helicopter or ship arrives — continue to ration food and water. The
rescue operation itself can be dangerous; great caution should be
taken. Survivors may need enough strength to climb a ladder or pull
themselves up to a rescue craft — save your strength, don't swim
unless necessary.

**Step off your survival craft onto the rescue craft only when the
survival craft is on the crest or top of a swell.** This will drop
your survival craft down while you're climbing the ladder or net;
otherwise there's danger of your boat/raft being raised up by the wave
and hitting you.

### Helicopter Hoist Procedures (p.134 bottom – p.138)

**Intro (p.134):** The wind developed by a helicopter's rotor system
can be over **seventy knots** — all loose gear on deck must be
securely tied down or stowed below, since loose objects blown into the
rotor could destroy it. A survivor's voice cannot be heard over
helicopter engine noise, so problems should be worked out before the
helicopter hovers overhead. "Don't forget to wear your life jacket!"
There are **four ways** a Coast Guard helicopter might rescue survivors
or evacuate injured mariners: (1) Rescue basket, (2) Rescue sling,
(3) Stokes litter, (4) Ship landing.

**Rescue Basket (p.135):** The U.S. Coast Guard usually uses a rescue
basket for survivors who can help themselves. Unless there is an
emergency, only one person at a time gets in the basket. Climb in
after it touches the deck of the ship or the water alongside the
raft/boat. **The pilot will touch the basket down first to discharge
static electricity.** Sit down, keep hands and arms inside. Each
basket has a small instruction card, but it's best to know how to use
it ahead of time — little time to read the card in an emergency.
(Illustration caption, not treated as a tested fact: a twin-turbine
HH-3F amphibious helicopter demonstrating a basket rescue — built by
Sikorsky, two 1,500 hp gas turbine engines, top speed 157 mph, range
700 miles.)

**Rescue Sling (p.135):** Carried onboard helicopters; used by rescue
helicopters from other countries more often than by the U.S. Coast
Guard. It's a padded loop placed over the body and underneath the
armpits, with the arms held around the sling.

**Stokes Litter (p.136):** Used to hoist those with serious injuries,
illnesses, or who are unable to walk. Loading procedure: get help from
other crew members; disconnect and spread out the straps; remove the
blankets; put the patient in and cover with the blankets; snugly
fasten the straps with the pad on top of the chest. If the litter must
be taken below decks to the patient, it must be unhooked from the
cable — **the cable hook must not be attached to any part of the
vessel**, since there's always a possibility of an emergency aboard the
helicopter itself requiring it to move unexpectedly (the pilot may
hover off to one side of the vessel while waiting to decrease this
danger).

**General hoist safety (p.136):** If a steadying line is attached to
the basket, rescue sling, or litter, it must be tended — this stops
the device from swinging too much. It is very important that the
rescue device touches the vessel before anyone touches it, since
static electricity (built up in the helicopter during flight) will
discharge as soon as the lowered object touches the deck — touching it
first could cause a shock that makes you let go with a hand you're
using for personal safety. **Never shine lights on the helicopter — it
will blind the pilot.** To signal the helicopter that all is ready for
hoisting, give a thumbs-up signal; the patient nods their head if
able. When approaching a helicopter in the water, be sure the crew
sees you (the pilot cannot see all around the aircraft) — it's best to
approach from ahead or from the starboard access door. If safe to do
so, deflate the roof of the liferaft to reduce the danger of the raft
overturning during a helicopter rescue. (Diagram: a circle around the
helicopter marks DESIRED [directly ahead], ACCEPTABLE [either side],
PROHIBITED [aft quarters], and DANGER [directly astern] approach
zones — labels fully legible.)

**Ship Landing (p.137):** Some very large merchant ships have a
helicopter landing area on deck — the helicopter actually lands on the
ship. A large **H** marks the landing area, e.g. on the fantail of a
tanker.

**Hoist Operations, 14-step numbered procedure (p.138):**
1. Move the patient as close to the hoist area as possible — time is
   important.
2. If a litter is required, move the patient to the special litter
   lowered by the helicopter as quickly as possible.
3. When the patient is in the helicopter's litter, be sure they're
   strapped in **face up**, with their life jacket on if condition
   permits.
4. Be sure the patient is tagged to indicate what medication, if any,
   was given and when.
5. Have the patient's medical records and any necessary papers ready
   in an envelope or package to transfer with them.
6. Change the ship's course so it rides as easily as possible, with
   the wind about **30° on the bow** (preferably the port bow) — this
   also keeps stack gases clear of the hoist area. Maintain the course
   once established.
7. Reduce the ship's speed if necessary to ease its motion, but
   maintain enough forward speed to respond to the helm. Maintain the
   speed once established.
8. Without radio contact, signal the helicopter in with a "come on"
   hand signal; at night, use a flashlight for the "come on" signal.
9. **To avoid static shock, allow the basket or litter to touch the
   deck before handling it.**
10. If a steadying line is dropped by the helicopter, guide the basket
    or litter to the deck with it, and keep the line clear at all
    times.
11. Place the patient in the basket sitting with hands clear of the
    sides, or in the litter as described earlier. Signal the hoist
    operator when ready — the patient nods their head if able, or deck
    personnel give a "thumbs up" signal.
12. If the litter must be taken away from the hoist area, unhook the
    hoist cable and keep it free so the helicopter can haul it in.
    **Do not secure the hoist cable to the ship or try to move the
    litter without unhooking it.** The hoist line should be slack
    before the basket or stretcher is reconnected.
13. When the patient is strapped in the litter, signal the helicopter
    to lower the hoist cable, hook it up, and signal the hoist
    operator when the litter is ready to lift.
14. If a steadying line is attached, use it to keep the basket or
    litter from turning or swinging — keep your feet clear of this
    line.

### Medical Evacuation (p.139, single page)

Occasionally helicopters evacuate ill or injured crew members from a
ship at sea to a hospital. The ship's master has a complete check-off
list to get the ship and crew ready to meet the helicopter:

**Requesting Helicopter Assistance:**
1. Give the ship's accurate position, speed and course, the time,
   weather conditions, wind direction and velocity, radio frequencies,
   etc.
2. Give complete medical information on the victim, including whether
   or not they are ambulatory (able to move on their own).
3. If the ship is beyond the helicopter's range, tell the rescuers
   where the ship is going so a meeting point can be arranged.
4. Advise the rescuers of any other changes immediately. If the
   patient dies before the helicopter arrives, notify them as soon as
   possible — the helicopter crew is risking their lives to help you.

**Preparations Prior to Arrival of the Helicopter:**
1. Provide continuous radio guard on Channel 16.
2. Select and clear a hoist area — aft if possible, with a **minimum
   fifty-foot radius**. Secure loose gear, awnings, and antenna wires;
   trice up running rigging and booms. If the hoist area is aft, lower
   the flagstaff. If possible, fly flags (not near the hoist area) to
   give the pilot a visual wind reference; at night, shine a light on
   the flag.
3. If the hoist is at night, light the lift area as well as possible.
   **Do not shine any lights on the helicopter**, so the pilot won't
   be blinded. Light any obstructions in the hoist area.
4. Point searchlights at a vertical angle to help the pilot locate the
   ship; secure them when the helicopter arrives.
5. Tell the pilot the location of the hoist area **before** the
   helicopter arrives so they can plan their approach.
6. Arrange a set of hand signals among the crew to help with the lift,
   since the helicopter's high noise level means voice commands can't
   be heard.

### Dangers Involved with Training, Drills and Maintenance (p.140–141)

There's no substitute for hands-on, realistic, pertinent training and
drills. Maintenance and servicing must be carried out on life-saving
appliances. Recent studies/publications have identified seafarer
fatalities and serious injuries from **testing of lifeboats** —
regulations require frequent movement of lifeboats, and lifeboats and
launching systems/davits require frequent maintenance, which is why
understanding proper safety procedures before testing and maintenance
matters. Consistent drills and training make these practices "second
nature" during a real emergency.

**Three most common causes of maritime accidents involving lifeboats:**
- **Design Failure** — faults in IMO-required off-load/on-load release
  systems are a frequent contributor to lifeboat accidents; the IMO
  continues modifying requirements to make these devices safer.
- **Lack of Maintenance** — survival craft systems sit in an
  unhospitable marine environment and require frequent maintenance;
  equipment failure results from lack of proper, timely maintenance.
- **Lack of Proper Training** — due to the variety of manufacturers and
  installations, it's vital to understand how the releasing gear on
  *your* ship operates — don't assume all gear operates the same, they
  don't.

**Fall Preventer Device (FPD) (p.140):** Most modern lifeboat
installations incorporate a means to prevent the lifeboat from
releasing while maintenance is being conducted, called a Fall
Preventer Device. It should be used whenever entering a lifeboat under
non-emergency conditions for maintenance. FPDs are designed to "safe"
the releasing gear so it won't release accidentally — they use slings
and shackles between the fall block ring and a shackle on the
releasing gear.

**Locking/Maintenance Pin (p.141):** With the pin inserted into the
fore/aft releasing gear, the bale cannot move, preventing accidental
release. When either an FPD or locking pin is used, it's imperative to
remove the safety device once maintenance is completed — otherwise the
lifeboat won't be able to release in an emergency.

**Practical safety measures for lifeboat maintenance/entry (p.141):**
- Never enter or conduct maintenance on a lifeboat without notifying
  and getting permission from the Officer of the Watch/Bridge — they
  may know of circumstances making entry unsafe, and need to know when
  the boat is made safe for maintenance and later returned to
  ready-for-emergency status.
- Wear proper PPE: a hardhat, gloves, and a PFD (typically a Type IV
  Buoyant Work Vest).
- Make the lifeboat "safe" for drills/maintenance using an FPD, a
  Maintenance Pin, or both.
- Upon entering, immediately check the state of the releasing gear —
  handle in the "green" (or equivalent "locked") position with safety
  pins inserted.
- After completing drill/maintenance, remove the FPD and/or
  Locking/Maintenance Pin.
- Notify the OOW/Bridge that the lifeboat has been returned to ready
  status — in all respects ready to be launched.
