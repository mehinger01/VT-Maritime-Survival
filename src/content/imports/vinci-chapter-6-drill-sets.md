# Chapter 6 Targeted Drill Sets (Recommended Groupings of Existing Questions)

**Status: drill-set recommendations only. Every question ID below
already exists live in `src/content/course.json` — no question was
created, edited, or promoted to produce this document. These are
suggested groupings for focused practice, not a new content type or a
change to the app's data model.**

Each drill set below groups existing Chapter 6 question IDs around one
specific point of confusion, so a student (or the app, if this pattern
is ever wired into a "drill mode" UI) can practice just that distinction
in a short, focused round rather than the full 55-question chapter pool.

---

## 1. Choosing the Right Hoist Device

**Purpose:** Four different rescue methods (basket, sling, litter, ship
landing) each match a different survivor condition — this drill builds
the skill of matching device to situation, not just recalling that each
device exists.

**Related concepts:** self-help vs. cannot-help-themselves survivors;
the rescue sling's requirement that the survivor actively hold on
(and why that makes it wrong for a hypothermic patient); Stokes litter
loading procedure and cable-hook safety; one-at-a-time basket boarding.

**Recommended existing question IDs:**
`q-ch6man-four-hoist-methods`, `q-ch6man-basket-one-at-a-time`,
`q-rescue-7`, `q-ch6man-sling-description`,
`q-ch6man-sling-usage-comparison`, `q-ch6man-litter-usage`,
`q-ch6man-litter-loading-procedure`, `q-ch6man-litter-cable-hook-safety`,
`q-ch6man-ship-landing-marking`, `q-ch6-rescuesling-official-hard`,
`q-rescue-4`

**Misconception this drill corrects:** Treating "basket," "sling," and
"litter" as interchangeable ways to say "the hoist thing." Each has a
specific intended survivor condition, and picking the wrong one for an
incapacitated patient (the sling, specifically) is a real, tested
scenario — not just a vocabulary distinction.

## 2. Hoist Operations Sequence & Safety

**Purpose:** The 14-step Hoist Operations procedure is dense with
specific figures and two explicit safety warnings — this drill isolates
the sequence and its two "do not" rules from the broader device
knowledge in Drill 1.

**Related concepts:** ship course/speed setup (30° on the bow); patient
strapping, tagging, and paperwork; the "come on" hand/flashlight
signal; static-discharge timing; the hoist-cable-not-secured-to-ship
rule; steadying-line handling; never shining lights on the helicopter;
safe water approach direction; liferaft roof deflation.

**Recommended existing question IDs:**
`q-ch6man-hoist-ship-course-angle`, `q-ch6man-hoist-litter-strapping`,
`q-ch6man-hoist-patient-tagging`, `q-ch6man-hoist-medical-records`,
`q-ch6man-hoist-comeon-signal`, `q-ch6man-hoist-cable-not-secured`,
`q-ch6man-hoist-ready-signal`, `q-ch6man-hoist-steadying-line-feet`,
`q-ch6man-steadying-line-purpose`, `q-ch6man-never-shine-lights-helicopter`,
`q-ch6man-approach-helicopter-in-water`, `q-ch6man-deflate-liferaft-roof`,
`q-ch6man-rotor-wind-speed`, `q-rescue-2`, `q-rescue-9`

**Misconception this drill corrects:** Assuming any well-intentioned
action (securing the cable to "keep it safe," touching the basket
early to "help it along") is harmless. Several steps in this procedure
exist specifically to prevent an instinctive but wrong action — this
drill trains recognizing which instinct to override.

## 3. Medical Evacuation Checklist

**Purpose:** A comprehensive checklist drill covering both halves of
the Medical Evacuation section — what to communicate when first
requesting a helicopter, and what to physically prepare before it
arrives.

**Related concepts:** required information when requesting assistance
(position, course, weather, medical status, range limitations, and
changes); hoist-area selection and size; wind-reference flags;
night-lighting rules; searchlight technique; noise-driven hand signals.

**Recommended existing question IDs:**
`q-ch6man-medevac-request-info`, `q-ch6man-medevac-ambulatory-info`,
`q-ch6man-medevac-beyond-range`, `q-ch6man-medevac-notify-changes`,
`q-ch6man-medevac-radio-guard`, `q-ch6man-medevac-hoist-area-specs`,
`q-ch6man-medevac-flags-wind-reference`, `q-ch6man-medevac-night-lighting`,
`q-ch6man-medevac-searchlight-use`, `q-ch6man-medevac-hand-signals`

**Misconception this drill corrects:** Conflating "light the area" with
"light the helicopter" (opposite instructions), and underestimating how
much specific information (not just "we have a sick crew member") a
proper medevac request requires.

## 4. Lifeboat Maintenance Safety

**Purpose:** A distinct safety domain from the rest of the chapter —
this is about protecting the crew doing routine lifeboat maintenance,
not about being rescued. Groups the accident-cause framework with the
specific devices and habits that prevent those accidents.

**Related concepts:** the three common causes of lifeboat accidents
(design failure, lack of maintenance, lack of training); the
Locking/Maintenance Pin; OOW/Bridge notification before entry; PPE;
checking releasing-gear state on entry; returning the boat to ready
status afterward.

**Recommended existing question IDs:**
`q-ch6man-lifeboat-accident-causes`, `q-ch6man-locking-pin-purpose`,
`q-ch6man-maintenance-notify-oow`, `q-ch6man-maintenance-ppe`,
`q-ch6man-maintenance-check-releasing-gear`,
`q-ch6man-maintenance-return-to-ready`

**Misconception this drill corrects:** Assuming lifeboat danger only
exists during a real emergency launch. Real seafarer injuries and
fatalities have come from routine testing and maintenance specifically
— this drill trains the habits (notify, PPE, check, remove-when-done)
that prevent that.

**Note:** The Fall Preventer Device (FPD) itself is not included in
this drill set — `q-ch6man-fpd-purpose` and `q-ch6man-fpd-mechanism`
remain held pending secondary verification (see
`vinci-2026-07-18-chapter-6-promotion-review.md`) and are not yet live.
The FPD concept is still taught in this chapter's Learn Mode content,
which explicitly distinguishes it from the already-live "preventer
bars" question.

## 5. Follow the Rescue Crew

**Purpose:** A general-coordination-principle drill, distinct from the
manual-specific procedures in Drills 1–4. These questions test *why*
survivors should defer to the rescue crew's judgment across several
different scenarios, rather than any one specific manual passage.

**Related concepts:** crew-directed pace and timing; not interfering
with rescue equipment; staying calm and following instructions from a
rescue swimmer; using a thrown heaving line as-is; prioritizing people
over property during an active rescue; why equipment knowledge doesn't
equal situational command authority.

**Recommended existing question IDs:**
`q-rescue-1`, `q-rescue-3`, `q-rescue-5`, `q-rescue-6`, `q-rescue-8`,
`q-rescue-10`, `q-rescue-11`, `q-rescue-12`

**Misconception this drill corrects:** Believing that confidence,
equipment knowledge, or good intentions justify a survivor acting
independently during an active rescue. Every question in this set
reinforces the same underlying answer — defer to the trained crew —
across enough different scenarios that the principle generalizes,
rather than being memorized as one specific case.
