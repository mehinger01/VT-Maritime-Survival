**Slide 2 — Liferaft Size.**
- Types: Inflatable, Rigid Liferaft, Davit-launched.
- Must be USCG approved and inspected annually at a USCG-approved
  facility.
- Capacity (number of people) marked on the side of the raft container
  on a placard/sticker.
- A liferaft that may have to be moved for launching cannot exceed
  **400 lbs**.
- Liferafts launched directly from their stowed position have **no
  weight limit**.

---

**Slide 3 — Liferaft Equipment.**
- Equipment for maneuvering: sea anchor, paddles.
- Sea anchors must be attached at a position orienting the primary
  entrance away from the seas as far as practicable, while still
  allowing retrieval by a person inside the raft.
- **Paddles must be at least 4 ft. long**, same size and type.
- Rafts are less maneuverable than lifeboats (large sail area, shallow
  draft, shape) — harder to handle than lifeboats.
- High wind can capsize the raft if the crew doesn't rig a sea anchor.
- **Exam-emphasis language:** none flagged explicitly, but the
  paddle-length figure matches an already-tested fact (see reconciliation).

**Slide 10 — Liferaft Stowage.**
- Kept in cradles on an open deck.
- Each liferaft container must be stowed with the words **"THIS SIDE
  UP"** on top.
- Container held together with packing bands.
- Container secured to the cradle with a tie-down strap.
- Tie-down strap held with a hydrostatic release.
- A cleat on deck is used to secure the sea painter/operating cord when
  launched manually.

---

**Slide 11 — Liferaft Design.**
- Able to be towed at **3 knots** in calm water without capsizing.
- CO2 (carbon dioxide) inflates the raft; cylinder is on the bottom of
  the raft. **Gas is colorless and odorless — watch for leaks.**
- Normal for gas to escape right after inflation; pressure relief valves
  let excess air escape.
- Buoyancy tubes: located on the outer edge, thick nylon-reinforced
  rubber, divided into two compartments. **Will hold the rated number of
  persons even if half the compartments are inflated.**
- Deflation plugs provided to deflate the raft after rescue.

---

**Slide 12 — (Liferaft Design, continued — no separate title).**
- During the day, rising temperature expands the CO2; the PRV may bleed
  off excess pressure in the buoyancy tubes.
- At night, dropping temperature may require pumping up the buoyancy
  tubes with the hand pump.
- The floor may be inflatable: in cold weather, inflated, it adds
  insulation; in warm weather, deflated, it helps cool the inside.
- Some floors can be removed and used as an extra float.

---

**Slide 13 — (Liferaft Design, continued — no separate title).**
- A boarding ladder and towing bridle are fitted at **each end** of the
  raft.
- The raft can be hoisted onto a ship by hooking onto one or both towing
  bridles.
- Life lines are provided **inside and outside** the raft.
- Two lights are installed on the canopy, **automatically activated at
  inflation**, operate for **12 hours**, visible from **2 miles** away.

---

**Slide 14 — (Liferaft Design, continued — no separate title).**
- The canopy has two layers for temperature protection, pops up
  automatically at inflation, is **International Orange**.
- Ballast bags/water pockets located under the floor, with holes to let
  seawater fill them. Two purposes: (1) slow the raft's drift, (2) make
  the raft more stable and less likely to capsize.

**Slide 23 — Automatic Launching.**
- The Hydrostatic release is triggered by water pressure. **The ship
  must sink 5-15 feet deep before the release operates.** The container
  then floats free toward the surface.
- The painter/operating cord is attached by the weak link to the cradle.

---

**Slide 24 — (Automatic Launching, continued — no separate title).**
- The other end of the painter/operating cord is attached to the raft's
  inflation tank and the towing bridle.
- As the ship sinks, most of the sea painter/operating cord is pulled
  out; this activates the inflation system and the raft inflates.
- The raft's buoyancy breaks the weak link, freeing the raft from the
  ship.

---

**Slide 25 — Length of Painter/Operating Cord.**
- The painter for a rigid liferaft varies depending on the height of
  stowage above the light waterline.
- Liferafts will have a painter that is **at least 10 meters plus the
  distance from the stowed position to the light waterline, or 15
  meters, whichever is greater.**
- **The operating cord has a length of at least 100 ft.**
- Worked example in the diagram: "Length of painter 50m/164 feet, Max
  stowage height 36m/118 feet." (Checked: 10m + 36m = 46m minimum; the
  example's 50m is a longer-than-minimum actual length, not a
  contradiction of the formula.)

**Slide 26 — Manual Launching.**
- To manually launch: trip the pelican hook/plunger pin on the
  hydrostatic release to release the tie-down strap. **The pelican
  hook/plunger pin can be released while under strain.**
- Take the container to the **leeward** side of the ship, tie the
  operating cord to a secure object, and throw the container over the
  side.
- Pull the remaining length of sea painter/operating cord to inflate the
  raft.

---

**Slide 27 — Righting the Capsized Raft.**
- A righting strap is fitted on the underneath side of the raft.
- Swim to the side marked **"Right Here"** — the side where the CO2
  cylinder is located.
- The wind should be blowing **towards you**.
- Reach up and grab the righting strap. Lean back and pull. **Continue
  pulling and pull yourself free of the raft. DO NOT LET GO!!!**
- **Exam-emphasis language:** "DO NOT LET GO!!!" is capitalized and
  triple-punctuated in the original — a clear instructor emphasis cue.

**Slide 32 — (righting/self-extraction guidance, continued — no separate

- Maintain contact with the raft at all times.
- The raft will fall over on top of you but will not harm you.
- Use the righting strap to work your way out from underneath the raft.
- Photo labels: "Rain water catchment," "External Lifelines," "Towing

**Slide 33 — Boarding the Liferaft.**
- Liferafts may be boarded: at the embarkation deck (davit-launched
  raft); by climbing down the Jacob's ladder; **by jumping into the
  canopy opening, 4 ft or less — NOT RECOMMENDED!!!**; from the sea with
  the boarding ladder.

---

**Slide 34 — (Boarding the Liferaft, continued — no separate title).**
- Stay as dry as possible when getting off the vessel. **Do not jump
  onto the top of the canopy arch — if people are inside they will be
  injured.**
- Equipment stowed in the raft may injure you when jumping onto the
  canopy.
- When boarding from the sea: place feet on the boarding ladder, reach
  inside and grab the internal lifelines (helps steady yourself in rough
  water), pull yourself headfirst into the raft. **Do not grab the
  canopy — it will tear easily.**

---

**Slide 35 — Assisting Injured People.**
A 6-step procedure for two rescuers boarding an injured person:
1. Place outboard knees on top of buoyancy tubes.
2. Turn the injured person with their back towards the rescuers.
3. Grab the injured person's lifejacket with inboard hands.
4. Grab the injured person's upper arm with outboard hands.
5. Push the injured person slightly down in the water, using buoyancy
   to help spring them up and over into the liferaft, back first.
6. The 2 rescuers fall to either side on the floor; the injured person
   falls between them.
